│      [16:31] Lee: :face_palm:

The ACR1552U can work with your app, but it does *not behave the same way as built-in NFC on a phone/tablet*. The reader supports *PC/SC smart-card mode* and a separate *keyboard emulation mode*. ACS also says the keyboard-emulation configuration tool is for *Windows only*, while its iOS/iPadOS app support is via its own tools rather than native iPad NFC hardware.
So the key distinction is:
• *Built-in NFC flow:* your app is likely listening for NFC events through the device NFC APIs.
• *External reader flow:* the ACR1552U usually presents card data either through *PC/SC* integration or by *typing* data like a keyboard wedge. That is a different integration pattern. 
What that means for your app
If your app currently “works with built-in NFC,” then in most cases it will *not automatically work* with the ACR1552U as-is.
It may work with minimal changes only if:
your sign-in screen already has a focused input field and you are happy for the reader to *paste/type the card UID* into that field, like a barcode scanner. That would mean treating the reader as keyboard input rather than as NFC input. The ACR1552U explicitly supports *keyboard emulation mode*.
It will need more substantial changes if:
your app currently expects to receive NFC tag data directly from the device NFC framework and then parses records from that NFC session. In that setup, an external USB reader is a different source and your app would need a new integration path. The ACR1552U’s developer path is through its *PC/SC-compliant API/SDK*, not the iPad’s built-in NFC APIs.
Best practical route
For a school sign-in app, the easiest setup is usually:
1. Configure the ACR1552U to output the *UID* in keyboard emulation mode.
2. Add a dedicated hidden or visible input field in your app.
3. When the UID is typed in, your app looks up the student and completes the sign-in.
That is usually much simpler than trying to replicate your existing built-in NFC workflow exactly.
Important caveat
You also need to confirm that your student cards are compatible with the reader and that *UID output is enough* for your system. The ACR1552U supports common 13.56 MHz card families including ISO 14443 A/B, MIFARE, FeliCa, ISO 15693, and NFC tags.
My honest recommendation
Assume this:
> *Your current app will need at least some development work.*
Most likely:
• *small change* if you can accept reader-as-keyboard input
• *bigger change* if your app is tightly built around native NFC APIs
The cleanest approach is to adapt the app so the reader acts like a scanner, not like built-in NFC.
